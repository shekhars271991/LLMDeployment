INTERPRETATION

- N-gram speculation was strongly workload dependent. The high-reuse cohort
  benefited at every depth and context length.
- Ngram-8 produced the best high-reuse decode performance, ranging from
  +195.9% to +287.8% at the two longer contexts.
- The medium-reuse cohort slowed by approximately 6% to 8% in every n-gram
  mode. Proposal and verification overhead exceeded the value of accepted
  tokens for these prompts.
- The low-reuse cohort still showed modest gains, but these were much smaller
  than the gains for copying, code completion, and structured formatting.
- Short-context TTFT increased by about 16 ms under n-gram speculation.
  TTFT at 2,048 and 8,000 tokens remained close to control because prefill work
  dominates at longer contexts.
- Deeper speculation reduced token-level acceptance from 52.47% at depth 2 to
  23.47% at depth 8, while increasing the median accepted-token count from 510
  to 650. Acceptance rate alone therefore does not determine throughput.
- Ngram-8 had the best aggregate decode median for this nine-prompt mix, but it
  is not universally best: medium-reuse prompts were slower than control.
- Observed n-gram VRAM was lower than control in these runs. This is an
  observed process-level measurement and should not be treated as a general
  memory-saving guarantee.

VALIDITY AND PROVENANCE

- All modes used three complete benchmark runs.
- Every run completed 27 requests: 9 prompts at each of 3 exact input lengths.
- Prefix caching was disabled to prevent cross-request prefill reuse.
- Temperature, output limit, prompt set, context lengths, and concurrency were
  held constant.
- The first request after each n-gram server launch showed a warm-up TTFT
  outlier. Three-run cohort medians prevent one first-request outlier from
  determining the reported result.
- The original files timestamped 07:03:23, 11:28:04, and 11:30:18 were initially
  labeled ngram-2, but the corresponding vLLM server log proved that depth 4
  was active. Their filenames and internal labels were corrected to ngram-4.
- The later ngram-2 and ngram-8 filenames were verified against their server
  launch logs. Stale shell metadata embedded in those JSON files was corrected
  to match the verified server depths.

LIMITATIONS

- The benchmark is sequential and measures single-request latency, not
  throughput under concurrent production load.
- Some responses ended before 256 tokens because 256 was a maximum output
  length, not a forced completion length.
- Very short completions make decode-rate measurements more sensitive to
  timing overhead.
- The prompts and exact-length padding are synthetic and represent selected
  workload patterns rather than all production traffic.
- Acceptance metrics are aggregated across the whole run; they are not broken
  down by individual prompt or cohort.
